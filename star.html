01 — The Star

Proxima Centauri

A cool, dim red dwarf just 4.24 light-years from Earth, the closest star to our Sun and the anchor of the nearest known exoplanetary system.

The Closest Star

Proxima Centauri is a small M-type main-sequence star (spectral class M5.5Ve) located in the constellation Centaurus. Despite being the nearest star to the Solar System, it is far too faint to see with the naked eye, with an apparent magnitude of about 11.1. It was discovered in 1915 by Robert Innes at the Union Observatory in Johannesburg, South Africa, who recognized its exceptionally large proper motion across the sky[1].

Proxima is gravitationally bound to the Alpha Centauri binary system (Alpha Centauri A and B), orbiting the pair at a projected separation of roughly 13,000 AU, so far out that one orbit takes over half a million years[2]. Together the three stars make up the closest stellar system to Earth.

Stellar Properties

Proxima Centauri belongs to the most common class of stars in the Milky Way: M-dwarfs. These red dwarfs account for roughly 70% of all stars in the galaxy, yet none are visible to the unaided eye because they are so intrinsically faint. Understanding how Proxima compares to our Sun is essential for evaluating whether its planets could support conditions friendly to life.

Sun vs. Proxima: A Matter of Scale

The numbers in the table above can be difficult to intuit. Proxima Centauri has only about 12% of the Sun's mass, 15% of its radius, and a mere 0.17% of its luminosity. If you placed Proxima where the Sun is, Earth would receive roughly 600 times less light than it does today, and our sky would be dominated by a dim, deep-red star rather than the blazing yellow disk we know.

One notable consequence of Proxima's low mass is its long lifespan. The Sun will exhaust its core hydrogen fuel in roughly 10 billion years (it is about halfway through now). An M-dwarf like Proxima burns through its hydrogen so slowly that it will remain on the main sequence for trillions of years, far longer than the current age of the universe (13.8 Gyr). In principle, a habitable planet orbiting such a star would have an enormously long window of time for life to develop[3].

M-Dwarfs in Context

Our Solar System orbits a G-type star, but the Sun is actually somewhat unusual. The Milky Way contains an estimated 100–400 billion stars, and roughly 70% of them are M-dwarfs like Proxima Centauri[4]. Because M-dwarfs are so common, understanding whether their planets can be habitable is one of the central questions in exoplanet science today.

M-dwarfs differ from Sun-like stars in several ways that matter for planet habitability. Their habitable zones are much closer in (because the star is so dim, a planet must orbit nearby to receive enough warmth). This proximity creates challenges: the planet is more likely to be tidally locked (one face always toward the star), and it is exposed to intense stellar flares and high-energy radiation that can strip away atmospheric gases over time.

Stellar Activity and Flares

Proxima Centauri is classified as a flare star (variable type UV Ceti). It exhibits frequent, powerful flares that can temporarily increase its brightness by several times across UV and X-ray wavelengths. In March 2016, a superflare was observed that briefly made Proxima roughly 68 times brighter in the far-UV than its quiescent state[5].

This flare activity is driven by Proxima's strong magnetic field. M-dwarfs are fully or largely convective, meaning their interiors churn like a boiling pot from core to surface, which generates powerful, tangled magnetic fields. These fields store energy that is periodically released as flares.

For any planet orbiting close to Proxima, these flares represent a serious environmental hazard. Each burst delivers a pulse of extreme ultraviolet (XUV) radiation and energetic particles. Over billions of years, this bombardment could erode a planet's atmosphere through a process called atmospheric sputtering. Whether Proxima b can retain a substantial atmosphere despite this onslaught is one of the most active questions in the field, and we explore it in detail on the Habitability page.

Why Proxima Matters

Proxima Centauri's importance extends beyond its proximity. It represents the most accessible laboratory for studying planets around the most common type of star in the galaxy. Because it is so close, Proxima's planets are among the best candidates for future direct imaging and atmospheric characterization with next-generation telescopes like the Extremely Large Telescope (ELT).

In the broader context of exoplanet science, Proxima Centauri connects several of the themes explored in this course: the physics of stellar structure and how it determines what kinds of planets can form, the observational techniques that reveal those planets, and the conditions that govern whether a rocky world could be a place where life might take hold.
