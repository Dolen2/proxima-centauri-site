01 — The Star

Proxima Centauri

A cool, dim red dwarf 4.24 light-years from Earth, the closest star to the Sun.

The Closest Star

Proxima Centauri is a small M-type main-sequence star (spectral class M5.5Ve) in the constellation Centaurus. It is the nearest star to the Solar System but far too faint to see with the naked eye, at an apparent magnitude of about 11.1. Robert Innes identified it in 1915 at the Union Observatory in Johannesburg based on its large proper motion.

Proxima is gravitationally bound to the Alpha Centauri binary (Alpha Centauri A and B), orbiting the pair at roughly 13,000 AU. One orbit takes over half a million years. Together the three stars make up the closest stellar system to Earth.

Stellar Properties

Proxima Centauri is an M-dwarf, the most common class of star in the Milky Way. M-dwarfs account for roughly 70% of all stars in the galaxy, but none are visible to the naked eye. The table below compares Proxima to the Sun.

Sun vs. Proxima: A Matter of Scale

Proxima has about 12% of the Sun's mass, 15% of its radius, and 0.17% of its luminosity. Placed at the Sun's distance, it would deliver roughly 600 times less light to Earth.

Proxima's low mass also means a long lifespan. The Sun will exhaust its hydrogen in roughly 10 billion years; an M-dwarf like Proxima will remain on the main sequence for trillions of years, far longer than the current age of the universe (13.8 Gyr). A habitable planet around such a star would have an enormous window for life to develop.

M-Dwarfs in Context

The Sun is a G-type star, but G-dwarfs are a minority. The Milky Way contains an estimated 100–400 billion stars, and roughly 70% are M-dwarfs like Proxima. Whether M-dwarf planets can be habitable is one of the central questions in exoplanet science.

M-dwarfs differ from Sun-like stars in ways that matter for habitability. Their habitable zones are much closer in, since the star is so dim that a planet must orbit nearby to stay warm. This proximity makes tidal locking likely and exposes the planet to intense stellar flares and high-energy radiation that can strip atmospheric gases over time.

Stellar Activity and Flares

Proxima Centauri is classified as a flare star (UV Ceti variable). It flares frequently, with events that can increase its UV and X-ray brightness by several times. A superflare in March 2016 briefly raised its far-UV output by a factor of ~68[1].

This activity is driven by Proxima's strong magnetic field. M-dwarfs are fully or largely convective—their interiors churn from core to surface, generating tangled magnetic fields that store energy and release it as flares.

For a close-in planet, each flare delivers a pulse of extreme ultraviolet (XUV) radiation and energetic particles that can erode the atmosphere through atmospheric sputtering. Whether Proxima b retains any atmosphere after billions of years of this bombardment is explored on the Habitability page.

Why Proxima Matters

Proxima is the nearest system with a planet in the habitable zone, making its planets the most accessible targets for direct imaging and atmospheric characterization with next-generation telescopes like the ELT.

Proxima also ties together several course themes: stellar structure and its effect on planet formation, the observational techniques that detect those planets, and the conditions that determine whether a rocky world could support life.
