05 — The Future

What Comes Next

The telescopes and missions that could transform Proxima b from a data point into a world we can actually characterize.

The Direct Imaging Challenge

Today, everything we know about Proxima b comes from indirect methods, measuring the star's wobble to infer the planet's presence. The next frontier is direct imaging: collecting photons from the planet itself, separate from the overwhelming glare of the host star.

The angular separation between Proxima b and its star, as seen from Earth, is:

θ = a / d = 0.0485 AU / 1.301 pc ≈ 0.037 arcseconds = 37 mas Angular separation of Proxima b from its star

This is an extremely tight separation. The contrast ratio (planet flux vs. star flux) is roughly 10−7 in the mid-infrared. Achieving both the angular resolution and the contrast suppression needed to image Proxima b requires the largest telescopes equipped with advanced coronagraphs and adaptive optics.

Lecture 10 Connection — Direct Imaging (Bonus)

Lecture 10 covered the principles of direct imaging: angular resolution (θ ≈ 1.22 λ/D), coronagraphy to block starlight, and adaptive optics to correct atmospheric blurring. The inner working angle of a coronagraph determines the closest separation at which a planet can be detected. For Proxima b at 37 mas, this is achievable with ELT-class telescopes at thermal infrared wavelengths.

The Extremely Large Telescope

The European Southern Observatory's Extremely Large Telescope (ELT), currently under construction in Chile's Atacama Desert, will have a 39-meter primary mirror, making it the largest optical/infrared telescope ever built. Its diffraction limit at 10 μm is about 53 mas, and at 3.5 μm about 18 mas. This means the ELT has the angular resolution to resolve Proxima b from its star at mid-infrared wavelengths.

The ELT's planned mid-infrared instrument, METIS (Mid-infrared ELT Imager and Spectrograph), includes a coronagraphic mode specifically designed for high-contrast imaging of nearby exoplanets. Simulations suggest that METIS could detect thermal emission from Proxima b in the N-band (~10 μm) with a signal-to-noise ratio sufficient for a clear detection, assuming favorable atmospheric conditions[13].

A detection in thermal emission would immediately constrain Proxima b's temperature and provide the first direct evidence of whether the planet has a substantial atmosphere (an atmosphere would redistribute heat, changing the planet's thermal emission profile compared to a bare rock).

James Webb Space Telescope

JWST, launched in December 2021, is the most powerful space telescope ever deployed. While its 6.5-meter mirror lacks the angular resolution to directly image Proxima b (JWST's diffraction limit at 10 μm is ~330 mas, far larger than the 37 mas separation), JWST could contribute to characterizing the system in other ways.

If Proxima b transits (crosses in front of its star as seen from Earth), JWST's spectrographs could perform transmission spectroscopy, measuring the star's light filtered through the planet's atmosphere to identify molecular absorption features. However, the transit probability for Proxima b is only about 1.5% (due to its relatively wide orbit for transit geometry), and no transit has been convincingly detected to date.

Even without transits, JWST may be able to measure the thermal phase curve of Proxima b by tracking how the combined star+planet flux varies as the planet orbits, revealing the temperature contrast between the planet's dayside and nightside. This would provide constraints on heat redistribution and therefore on the presence and thickness of an atmosphere.

Breakthrough Starshot

The most ambitious proposal for studying Proxima Centauri is Breakthrough Starshot, a privately funded initiative announced in 2016, the same year Proxima b was discovered. The concept: launch thousands of ultra-lightweight spacecraft (gram-scale "StarChips") propelled by ground-based laser arrays to 20% the speed of light. At that velocity, the fleet would reach Alpha/Proxima Centauri in approximately 20 years.

Each StarChip would carry miniature cameras and instruments capable of taking close-up images of Proxima b as it flies past. The data would be transmitted back to Earth via onboard laser, arriving about 4.24 years later. The total mission time from launch to data reception: roughly 24 years.

The engineering challenges are enormous: surviving the acceleration phase, navigating interstellar dust at relativistic speeds, and building a laser array powerful enough to push the sails. As of 2026, the project remains in the research and development phase, but it represents the only plausible path to in-situ exploration of the Proxima system within this century.

What Would We Look For?

Whether through direct imaging from the ELT, thermal measurements from JWST, or an interstellar flyby, the key observational goals for Proxima b are:

Priority 1

Does It Have an Atmosphere?

The single most important question. Thermal emission measurements can distinguish between a bare rock (extreme day-night contrast) and an atmosphere (heat redistribution). Spectral features in reflected or emitted light would confirm atmospheric gases.

Priority 2

What Is It Made Of?

Atmospheric spectroscopy could reveal CO2, H2O, O2, O3, CH4, or other molecules. The composition constrains the planet's geological activity, volatile budget, and potential for biological processes.

Priority 3

Biosignatures

A simultaneous detection of O2 and CH4 (or O3 and N2O) would be difficult to explain without biological activity. This would constitute the strongest possible evidence for extraterrestrial life short of direct contact.

The Significance of Proximity

Proxima Centauri's location as the nearest star makes it uniquely valuable. Any technique that works on Proxima b (direct imaging, thermal mapping, spectroscopy) will be easier here than for any other known exoplanet of comparable interest. It is the first domino: if we can characterize Proxima b's atmosphere and surface conditions, the methods developed will extend to dozens of other nearby rocky worlds.

In the span of a single decade (2016–2026), Proxima b went from unknown to one of the most intensely studied planets in exoplanet science. The next decade, with the ELT's first light anticipated and JWST entering its prime mission phase, could transform it from a gravitational signal into a world we can truly begin to understand.
