05 — The Future

What Comes Next

Telescopes and missions that could turn Proxima b from a radial-velocity signal into a world with a measured atmosphere.

The Direct Imaging Challenge

Everything we know about Proxima b so far comes from the star's wobble. The next step is direct imaging: collecting photons from the planet itself. The angular separation between Proxima b and its star is:

θ = a / d = 0.0485 AU / 1.301 pc ≈ 0.037 arcseconds = 37 mas Angular separation of Proxima b from its star

At a contrast ratio of ~10−7 in the mid-infrared, imaging Proxima b requires the largest telescopes with advanced coronagraphs and adaptive optics.

ⓘ
Lecture 10 Connection — Direct Imaging (Bonus)

Lecture 10 covered the principles of direct imaging: angular resolution (θ ≈ 1.22 λ/D), coronagraphy to block starlight, and adaptive optics to correct atmospheric blurring. The inner working angle of a coronagraph determines the closest separation at which a planet can be detected. For Proxima b at 37 mas, this is achievable with ELT-class telescopes at thermal infrared wavelengths.

The Extremely Large Telescope

ESO's Extremely Large Telescope (ELT), under construction in Chile's Atacama Desert, will have a 39 m primary mirror. Its diffraction limit is ~53 mas at 10 μm and ~18 mas at 3.5 μm—enough to resolve Proxima b at 37 mas in the mid-infrared.

The ELT's METIS instrument includes a coronagraphic mode for high-contrast imaging of nearby exoplanets. Simulations suggest METIS could detect Proxima b's thermal emission in the N-band (~10 μm)[9].

A thermal detection would constrain the planet's temperature and test for an atmosphere: heat redistribution from dayside to nightside would shift the emission profile compared to a bare rock.

James Webb Space Telescope

JWST's 6.5 m mirror cannot resolve the 37 mas separation (its diffraction limit at 10 μm is ~330 mas), but it could characterize the system in other ways.

If Proxima b transits, JWST could perform transmission spectroscopy to identify atmospheric absorption features. The transit probability is only ~1.5%, and no transit has been detected so far.

Even without transits, JWST may measure the thermal phase curve —tracking how the combined star+planet flux varies over the orbit to reveal dayside-nightside temperature contrast and constrain atmospheric thickness.

Breakthrough Starshot

Breakthrough Starshot, announced in 2016, proposes launching thousands of gram-scale "StarChips" propelled by ground-based lasers to 20% of the speed of light. At that velocity, the fleet would reach Proxima Centauri in ~20 years. Data transmitted by onboard laser would arrive ~4 years later, for a total mission time of roughly 24 years.

The engineering challenges are large: surviving acceleration, navigating interstellar dust at relativistic speeds, and building a sufficiently powerful laser array. As of 2026 the project remains in R&D, but it is the only plausible path to in-situ exploration of the Proxima system within this century.

What Would We Look For?

Regardless of method, the key observational goals for Proxima b are:

Priority 1

Does It Have an Atmosphere?

The single most important question. Thermal emission measurements can distinguish between a bare rock (extreme day-night contrast) and an atmosphere (heat redistribution). Spectral features in reflected or emitted light would confirm atmospheric gases.

Priority 2

What Is It Made Of?

Atmospheric spectroscopy could reveal CO2, H2O, O2, O3, CH4, or other molecules. The composition constrains the planet's geological activity, volatile budget, and potential for biological processes.

Priority 3

Biosignatures

A simultaneous detection of O2 and CH4 (or O3 and N2O) would be difficult to explain without biological activity. This would constitute the strongest possible evidence for extraterrestrial life short of direct contact.

The Significance of Proximity

Any technique that works on Proxima b—direct imaging, thermal mapping, spectroscopy—will be easier here than for any other exoplanet of comparable interest. Methods proven on Proxima b will extend to dozens of other nearby rocky worlds.

Between 2016 and 2026, Proxima b went from unknown to one of the most studied exoplanets. The next decade, with ELT first light and JWST's extended mission, could turn it from a gravitational signal into a world with measured surface conditions.
