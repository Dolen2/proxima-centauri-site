Proxima d

0.26 M⊕ P = 5.12 d a = 0.029 AU

The lightest known planet in the system, roughly twice the mass of Mars. Proxima d orbits in only 5.12 days at 0.029 AU, well inside the inner edge of the habitable zone. At this proximity it receives about 2× the flux Earth gets from the Sun, making it likely too hot for surface liquid water. Its detection required the precision of the ESPRESSO spectrograph on ESO's VLT, which measured an RV semi-amplitude of just 0.39 m/s[8].

~1.03 R⊕
Habitable Zone

Proxima b

1.07 M⊕ P = 11.186 d a = 0.049 AU

The flagship world of the system. With a minimum mass of 1.07 M⊕, Proxima b is the closest known exoplanet to Earth in both distance and size. It orbits at 0.049 AU, placing it within Proxima's habitable zone. Questions remain about whether it can retain an atmosphere against Proxima's intense flare activity, and whether tidal locking creates extreme climate conditions. These are explored on the Habitability page.

~2.2 R⊕
Outermost

Proxima c

~7 M⊕ P ≈ 5.28 yr a = 1.49 AU

A super-Earth or mini-Neptune with a minimum mass around 7 M⊕, orbiting at 1.49 AU. This places Proxima c far beyond the habitable zone where temperatures are extremely cold (equilibrium temperature roughly 40 K). Its detection combined radial velocity data with astrometric measurements from Gaia, and there is tentative evidence from direct imaging that it may have a substantial ring or dust system[7].

Orbital Architecture

The Proxima system's architecture is extremely compact compared to our Solar System. Proxima b and d orbit closer to their star than Mercury orbits the Sun (0.387 AU). The entire inner system fits within a region smaller than 0.05 AU. Only Proxima c, at 1.49 AU, orbits at a distance comparable to Mars in our Solar System (1.52 AU), but around a star that is roughly 600 times fainter.

Verifying with Kepler's Third Law

We can check the consistency of the published orbital parameters using Kepler's Third Law, which relates orbital period P, semi-major axis a, and stellar mass M☆:

P² = (4π² / G M☆) · a³ Kepler's Third Law (planet mass negligible)

Rearranging to solve for the semi-major axis given the measured period and Proxima's mass (M☆ = 0.122 M⊙):

a = (G M☆ P² / 4π²)1/3

For Proxima b with P = 11.186 days = 9.665 × 105 s:

a = (6.674×10−11 · 2.426×1029 · (9.665×105)² / 4π²)1/3 ≈ 7.25 × 109 m ≈ 0.0485 AU Matches the published semi-major axis precisely

This confirms internal consistency: the published period and semi-major axis are exactly related by Newtonian gravity for Proxima's measured mass. The same check works for Proxima d and c.

Lecture 14 Connection — Planet Formation

In Lecture 14 we studied the nebular hypothesis: how planets form from a protoplanetary disk of gas and dust through core accretion and gravitational collapse. Forming rocky planets around M-dwarfs presents unique challenges. The protoplanetary disks of low-mass stars contain less total material, and the snow line sits much closer to the star. Simulations suggest that forming an Earth-mass planet like Proxima b at 0.05 AU may require significant inward migration from a formation site further out in the disk, where more solid material was available.

How Did These Planets Form?

The formation of the Proxima system is an active area of theoretical study. The standard core accretion model predicts that rocky planets grow by accumulating solid material: first micron-sized dust grains collide and stick, building up to kilometer-sized planetesimals, which then gravitationally attract more material to become protoplanets.

Around a star as small as Proxima Centauri, the protoplanetary disk likely contained only about 1–10% of the solid mass found in a Solar-type disk. This makes forming massive planets in situ more difficult, and suggests two possible formation scenarios for Proxima b:

In-situ formation with enhanced solids: If Proxima's disk had a higher-than-average dust-to-gas ratio (consistent with Proxima's slightly super-solar metallicity of [Fe/H] = +0.21), enough solid material may have been present in the inner disk to build a 1 M⊕ planet locally.

Formation further out and migration inward: Proxima b may have formed beyond the snow line (where water ice boosted the available solid mass) and migrated inward through gravitational interactions with the gas disk or with other forming planets. If this occurred, Proxima b could be volatile-rich, potentially harboring a significant water reservoir beneath (or instead of) a rocky surface.

The outer planet Proxima c, with its minimum mass of ~7 M⊕ at 1.49 AU, may have formed near or beyond the snow line through a combination of core accretion and pebble accretion, rapidly sweeping up centimeter-sized pebbles drifting inward through the disk.
