02 — Discovery

Finding the Planets

How a tiny periodic wobble in Proxima Centauri's motion revealed an Earth-mass planet in the habitable zone, using the radial velocity method.

The Radial Velocity Method

A planet does not simply orbit its star. The star and planet both orbit their common center of mass. For a system like Proxima Centauri b, the star is vastly more massive than the planet, so the center of mass sits very close to the star's core. But the star still moves. It traces a small circle (or ellipse) in response to the planet's gravitational pull, producing a periodic shift in the wavelengths of light we receive from it.

When the star moves toward Earth, its light is blue-shifted (compressed to shorter wavelengths). When it moves away, the light is red-shifted (stretched to longer wavelengths). By measuring these tiny Doppler shifts with a high-resolution spectrograph, astronomers can infer the presence of an orbiting planet, determine its orbital period, and estimate its minimum mass.

Lecture 6 & 7 Connection — Radial Velocities

Lectures 6 and 7 introduced the radial velocity technique: the Doppler effect applied to starlight, the concept of the center-of-mass velocity, and how the semi-amplitude K relates to the planet's orbital parameters and minimum mass (m sin i). The discovery of Proxima b is a textbook application of these principles.

From Wobble to Mass

The radial velocity semi-amplitude K (the maximum speed of the star's wobble toward or away from us) is related to the planet's properties by:

K = (2πG / P)1/3 · (mp sin i) / (M☆ + mp)2/3 · 1 / √(1 − e²) Radial velocity semi-amplitude

Where:

  • K = radial velocity semi-amplitude (m/s)
  • P = orbital period
  • mp sin i = minimum planet mass (we only measure the component along our line of sight)
  • M☆ = stellar mass
  • e = orbital eccentricity
  • i = orbital inclination (unknown from RV alone)

The critical limitation is the sin i degeneracy. Radial velocity measurements only detect the component of the star's motion along our line of sight. If we are viewing the orbit face-on (i = 0°), there is no radial component at all and the planet would be undetectable. If the orbit is edge-on (i = 90°), we measure the full amplitude. Since we typically do not know the inclination, the RV method gives us only a minimum mass: mp sin i.

Applying This to Proxima b

For Proxima b, the measured semi-amplitude is K ≈ 1.38 m/s with a period of P = 11.186 days[6]. Using Proxima's mass of M☆ = 0.122 M⊙ and assuming a nearly circular orbit (e ≈ 0.02), we can solve for the minimum mass:

mp sin i ≈ 1.07 M⊕ Minimum mass of Proxima b (essentially Earth-mass)

This tells us Proxima b has at minimum about the same mass as Earth. If the orbit is inclined at, say, 60°, the true mass would be about 1.24 M⊕. Even in the worst realistic cases, Proxima b is almost certainly a rocky, terrestrial-class planet.

Discovery Timeline

The road to confirming Proxima b stretched over more than a decade of observations. Here are the key milestones:

1915

Proxima Centauri discovered

Robert Innes identifies Proxima as the closest known star to the Sun based on its large proper motion.

2000–2014

Early RV hints

UVES and HARPS spectrographs accumulate radial velocity data on Proxima. A periodic signal begins to emerge but lacks sufficient significance for a definitive claim.

2016 (January–March)

Pale Red Dot campaign

A dedicated 60-night observing campaign with HARPS at La Silla Observatory targets Proxima nightly, building up the signal-to-noise ratio needed for confirmation.

2016 (August 24)

Proxima b announced

Anglada-Escudé et al. publish the discovery in Nature: a planet with m sin i ≈ 1.27 M⊕ (later refined to ~1.07 M⊕) orbiting in the habitable zone with P = 11.186 days[6].

2020

Proxima c confirmed

Damasso et al. report a second planet, Proxima c, with a minimum mass of about 7 M⊕ and an orbital period of ~5.2 years[7].

2022

Proxima d detected

Faria et al. announce Proxima d using ESPRESSO, a sub-Earth candidate with m sin i ≈ 0.26 M⊕ and an orbital period of just 5.12 days[8].

Challenges of RV Around M-Dwarfs

Detecting planets via radial velocity around a star like Proxima Centauri is not straightforward. M-dwarfs present several observational challenges:

Stellar activity noise. Proxima's frequent flares and starspots create spurious RV signals that can mimic or mask genuine planetary signals. Disentangling the planet's Doppler signature from activity-induced jitter was one of the main difficulties the Pale Red Dot team had to overcome. They used activity indicators (measurements of chromospheric emission lines like Hα) to model and subtract the stellar noise from the data.

Faintness. Even though Proxima is the closest star, its apparent magnitude of 11.1 makes it invisible to the naked eye. Gathering enough photons for precise RV measurements requires long exposures on large telescopes equipped with state-of-the-art spectrographs like HARPS (High Accuracy Radial velocity Planet Searcher).

Small signals. The 1.38 m/s semi-amplitude of Proxima b's signal is tiny (roughly the walking speed of a tortoise). Measuring Doppler shifts at this level demands sub-m/s instrumental precision sustained over months of observations.
