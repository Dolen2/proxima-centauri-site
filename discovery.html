02 — Discovery

Finding the Planets

How a tiny periodic wobble in Proxima Centauri's motion revealed an Earth-mass planet in the habitable zone, using the radial velocity method.

The Radial Velocity Method

A planet does not simply orbit its star—both bodies orbit their common center of mass. For Proxima b, the star is so much more massive that the center of mass sits near the star's core. But the star still moves, tracing a small circle (or ellipse) that produces a periodic shift in the wavelengths of light we receive.

When the star moves toward Earth, its light is blue-shifted; when it moves away, the light is red-shifted. Measuring these tiny Doppler shifts with a high-resolution spectrograph reveals the planet's presence, orbital period, and minimum mass.

ⓘ
Lecture 6 & 7 Connection — Radial Velocities

Lectures 6 and 7 introduced the radial velocity technique: the Doppler effect applied to starlight, the concept of the center-of-mass velocity, and how the semi-amplitude K relates to the planet's orbital parameters and minimum mass (m sin i). The discovery of Proxima b is a textbook application of these principles.

From Wobble to Mass

The radial velocity semi-amplitude K (the maximum speed of the star's wobble toward or away from us) is related to the planet's properties by:

K = (2πG / P)1/3 · (mp sin i) / (M☆ + mp)2/3 · 1 / √(1 − e²) Radial velocity semi-amplitude

Where:

  • K = radial velocity semi-amplitude (m/s)
  • P = orbital period
  • mp sin i = minimum planet mass (we only measure the component along our line of sight)
  • M☆ = stellar mass
  • e = orbital eccentricity
  • i = orbital inclination (unknown from RV alone)

The critical limitation is the sin i degeneracy. RV only detects the star's motion along our line of sight. A face-on orbit (i = 0°) produces no radial signal; an edge-on orbit (i = 90°) gives the full amplitude. Without knowing the inclination, RV yields only a minimum mass: mp sin i.

Applying This to Proxima b

The original 2016 discovery measured a semi-amplitude of K ≈ 1.38 m/s with a period of P = 11.186 days[2]. Using Proxima's mass of M☆ = 0.122 M⊙ and assuming a nearly circular orbit (e ≈ 0.02), solving the RV equation gives:

mp sin i ≈ 1.27 M⊕ Minimum mass from the 2016 Pale Red Dot campaign

ESPRESSO later revised this downward: K ≈ 1.20 m/s, giving mp sin i ≈ 1.07 M⊕ (the current accepted value). At an inclination of 60° the true mass would be about 1.24 M⊕. In nearly all realistic geometries, Proxima b is a rocky, terrestrial-class planet.

Discovery Timeline

The road to confirming Proxima b stretched over more than a decade of observations. Here are the key milestones:

1915

Proxima Centauri discovered

Robert Innes identifies Proxima as the closest known star to the Sun based on its large proper motion.

2000–2014

Early RV hints

UVES and HARPS spectrographs accumulate radial velocity data on Proxima. A periodic signal begins to emerge but lacks sufficient significance for a definitive claim.

2016 (January–March)

Pale Red Dot campaign

A dedicated 60-night observing campaign with HARPS at La Silla Observatory targets Proxima nightly, building up the signal-to-noise ratio needed for confirmation.

2016 (August 24)

Proxima b announced

Anglada-Escudé et al. publish the discovery in Nature: a planet with m sin i ≈ 1.27 M⊕ orbiting in the habitable zone with P = 11.186 days[2]. Later refined to ~1.07 M⊕ by ESPRESSO.

2020

Proxima c confirmed

Damasso et al. report a second planet, Proxima c, with a minimum mass of about 7 M⊕ and an orbital period of ~5.2 years[3].

2022

Proxima d detected

Faria et al. announce Proxima d using ESPRESSO, a sub-Earth candidate with m sin i ≈ 0.26 M⊕ and an orbital period of just 5.12 days[4].

Challenges of RV Around M-Dwarfs

Detecting planets via RV around an M-dwarf like Proxima is difficult for several reasons:

Stellar activity noise. Proxima's flares and starspots create spurious RV signals that can mimic or mask a planet. The Pale Red Dot team used chromospheric emission indicators (Hα) to model and subtract this jitter.

Faintness. At magnitude 11.1, Proxima requires long exposures on large telescopes with spectrographs like HARPS to gather enough photons for precise RV measurements.

Small signals. The ~1.3 m/s semi-amplitude is roughly the walking speed of a tortoise. Measuring Doppler shifts this small demands sub-m/s precision sustained over months.
