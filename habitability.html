04 — Habitability Analysis

Could Life Survive on Proxima b?

Equilibrium temperature, habitable zone position, tidal locking, and atmospheric escape—the factors that determine whether Proxima b could hold liquid water.

1. How Much Light Does Proxima b Receive?

The insolation flux S is the stellar power per unit area at the planet's distance:

S = L☆ / (4π d²) Insolation flux (power per unit area at distance d)

We can express this relative to Earth's insolation (S⊕ = 1361 W/m²):

S / S⊕ = (L☆ / L⊙) × (1 AU / d)2

For Proxima b, with L☆ = 0.0017 L⊙ and d = 0.0485 AU:

S / S⊕ = 0.0017 × (1 / 0.0485)² = 0.0017 × 425.1 ≈ 0.722 Proxima b receives about 72% of the sunlight Earth receives

Proxima b receives less energy than Earth but more than Mars (~43%). In terms of raw insolation, liquid surface water is plausible if other conditions are met.

Insolation vs. Distance for the Sun and Proxima Centauri

Stellar flux falls off as 1/d². Despite Proxima being ~600× less luminous than the Sun, Proxima b orbits so close that it receives comparable insolation to Earth.

2. Equilibrium Temperature

The equilibrium temperature Teq is the temperature of a bare blackbody in thermal balance with the incoming starlight:

πRp² · S · (1 − A) = 4πRp² · σ Teq4 Energy balance: absorbed starlight = radiated thermal emission

Solving for Teq:

Teq = [S (1 − A) / (4σ)]1/4 Equilibrium temperature formula

Where A is the Bond albedo (fraction of incident light reflected). For Earth, A ≈ 0.30. Using this as a reference for Proxima b:

Teq = [0.722 × 1361 × (1 − 0.3) / (4 × 5.67×10−8)]1/4 ≈ 235 K (−38°C) Equilibrium temperature of Proxima b (assuming Earth-like albedo)

Earth's Teq is about 255 K; its actual surface temperature is 288 K, the extra 33 K coming from the greenhouse effect. Proxima b would need roughly 38 K of greenhouse warming to reach 273 K (0°C), comparable to Earth's 33 K. A modest CO2/H2O atmosphere could supply this.

3. Habitable Zone Boundaries

The habitable zone (HZ) is the distance range where a rocky planet with an Earth-like atmosphere could sustain liquid surface water. Using the flux boundaries from Kopparapu et al. (2013)[5]:

dinner = √(L☆ / Sinner) ,  douter = √(L☆ / Souter) HZ boundaries in AU

The critical detail is that Seff depends on the star's effective temperature, not just its luminosity. Kopparapu et al. provide polynomial corrections to the solar baseline values as a function of Teff. For Proxima at Teff = 3042 K, these corrections shift the flux boundaries significantly compared to the solar values: Sinner ≈ 0.85 S⊕ (moist greenhouse) and Souter ≈ 0.24 S⊕ (maximum greenhouse).

dinner = √(0.0017 / 0.85) ≈ 0.045 AU
douter = √(0.0017 / 0.24) ≈ 0.084 AU Conservative habitable zone of Proxima Centauri (M-dwarf corrected)

Proxima b orbits at 0.049 AU—inside the conservative HZ but only 0.004 AU from the inner edge. A modest increase in luminosity or decrease in albedo could push it out. For comparison, the Sun's HZ spans roughly 0.95–1.67 AU, about 20 times farther out.

Lecture 17 defined the habitable zone and explored the many factors beyond simple insolation that contribute to true habitability: atmospheric composition, magnetic fields, stellar activity, tidal effects, and geological activity. The habitable zone is a necessary but not sufficient condition for life. Proxima b meets the insolation criterion, but the challenges described below (tidal locking, flare exposure, and atmospheric escape) show why being "in the HZ" does not guarantee habitability.

4. Tidal Locking

At 0.049 AU, tidal forces on Proxima b are strong enough to slow its rotation until one face permanently points toward the star (tidal locking). The locking timescale can be estimated as[6]:

tlock ≈ (C · ω0 · a6) / (G M☆² Rp3) Approximate tidal locking timescale

Here C depends on rigidity and dissipation factor Q, ω0 is the initial spin rate, a the semi-major axis, and Rp the planet radius. For an Earth-like planet at Proxima b's distance:

tlock ∼ 108–109 years Much shorter than the system's age (~4.85 Gyr). Proxima b is almost certainly tidally locked

Since tlock scales as a6, the locking time at 0.049 AU is orders of magnitude shorter than the system age. Proxima b almost certainly has a permanent dayside and a permanent nightside.

Climate Implications

Tidal locking was once considered fatal to habitability, but global circulation models (GCMs) show that even a few hundred millibars of CO2 or N2 can transport enough heat to prevent atmospheric collapse[7]. Models of a tidally locked Proxima b with an ocean predict a temperate ring near the substellar point, with open water on the dayside and ice caps on the nightside.

5. Atmospheric Escape

The biggest threat to habitability is atmospheric erosion from Proxima's extreme ultraviolet (XUV) radiation and frequent flares.

ⓘ
Lecture 16 Connection — Planet Atmospheres

In Lecture 16 we studied how planetary atmospheres are structured and how they can be lost: thermal (Jeans) escape for light molecules at the exobase, hydrodynamic escape when XUV heating drives bulk outflow, and non-thermal processes like ion sputtering from stellar wind interactions. Proxima b is subject to all of these mechanisms at elevated rates due to its star's extreme activity.

XUV-Driven Escape

The dominant loss mechanism is energy-limited hydrodynamic escape. The mass-loss rate can be estimated as[8]:

dM/dt = (ε · π Rp² · FXUV) / (G Mp / Rp) Energy-limited mass loss rate

Here ε ~ 0.1–0.3 is the heating efficiency. Proxima's quiescent XUV luminosity is ~1027 erg/s; at 0.049 AU, the XUV flux on Proxima b is 30–250× Earth's[8]. With Earth-like parameters and ε = 0.15:

dM/dt ∼ 107–108 g/s Estimated atmospheric mass loss rate for Proxima b

Over 4.85 Gyr, this implies a total loss of 1–10 Earth ocean equivalents of hydrogen. Whether any atmosphere survives depends on the initial volatile budget: a planet that formed volatile-rich (migration scenario) may have weathered the storm; one that formed dry may have been stripped long ago.

Flare Erosion

Superflares add further erosion. The 2016 event raised UV flux by ~68×[1]. Cumulative flare effects over billions of years could strip tens of millibars per Gyr, enough to remove an Earth-like atmosphere if not replenished by outgassing.

6. Putting It All Together

The quantitative picture is genuinely ambiguous:

Factor Assessment Verdict
Insolation flux 72% of Earth's, within range for liquid water Favorable
Equilibrium temperature 235 K; needs ~38 K greenhouse to reach 273 K Plausible
Habitable zone position Within conservative HZ boundaries Favorable
Tidal locking Almost certainly locked, but GCMs show heat transport can prevent collapse Mixed
Atmospheric escape Severe XUV + flare erosion; 1–10 oceans of H lost over 4.85 Gyr Challenging
Magnetic field Unknown; would help shield atmosphere from stellar wind Unknown

The answer hinges on unknowns: magnetic field strength, initial volatile budget, and whether active volcanism replenishes atmospheric gases. Resolving these requires observations—the subject of the next page.

⚠
Key Insight

Being in the habitable zone is necessary but not sufficient. Proxima b passes the insolation test, but the interplay of tidal locking, stellar flares, and atmospheric escape makes the outcome genuinely uncertain. This is precisely why Proxima b is such a compelling target for future observations. It represents a real test case for the limits of habitability around the most common type of star in the galaxy.
